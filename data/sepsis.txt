SEPSIS AND SEPTIC SHOCK - COMPREHENSIVE CLINICAL REFERENCE
Based on Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021 Guidelines and Sepsis-3 Definitions

SECTION 1: DEFINITIONS AND CLASSIFICATION

What is sepsis?
Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. This definition, known as Sepsis-3, replaced the older SIRS-based definition in 2016. Sepsis is not simply infection — it requires evidence of organ dysfunction on top of suspected or confirmed infection.

What is the SOFA score?
The Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score measures organ dysfunction across six systems: respiratory (PaO2/FiO2 ratio), coagulation (platelets), liver (bilirubin), cardiovascular (MAP and vasopressor requirement), central nervous system (Glasgow Coma Scale), and renal (creatinine and urine output). An acute increase of 2 or more SOFA points indicates organ dysfunction consistent with sepsis.

What is qSOFA?
Quick SOFA (qSOFA) is a bedside screening tool with three criteria: altered mental status (Glasgow Coma Scale below 15), respiratory rate of 22 breaths per minute or greater, and systolic blood pressure of 100 mmHg or less. Two or more qSOFA criteria suggest possible sepsis and should prompt further evaluation. qSOFA is a screening tool, not a diagnostic criterion.

What is septic shock?
Septic shock is a subset of sepsis with profound circulatory and cellular/metabolic dysfunction. It is identified clinically by vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP of 65 mmHg or greater despite adequate fluid resuscitation, and serum lactate greater than 2 mmol/L. Hospital mortality in septic shock exceeds 40%.

What is the difference between sepsis and septic shock?
Sepsis is organ dysfunction due to infection. Septic shock is sepsis plus refractory hypotension requiring vasopressors and elevated lactate despite fluids. Septic shock represents a more severe phenotype with higher mortality risk. Not all sepsis patients develop septic shock.

SECTION 2: EPIDEMIOLOGY

How common is sepsis?
Sepsis affects approximately 1.7 million adults in the United States annually. It is a leading cause of hospital mortality, accounting for 270,000 deaths per year in the US alone. Globally, sepsis affects 49 million people annually and causes 11 million deaths, representing 20% of all global deaths.

Who is at highest risk for sepsis?
High-risk populations include elderly patients over 65, immunocompromised patients including those on chemotherapy or immunosuppressive therapy, patients with chronic conditions such as diabetes, cirrhosis, chronic kidney disease, and COPD, patients with recent surgery or invasive procedures, patients with indwelling catheters or devices, and neonates and infants under 1 year.

What are the most common sources of infection in sepsis?
The most common infection sources are pulmonary (pneumonia, 35-50%), abdominal (peritonitis, cholangitis, 25%), urinary tract (15-20%), bloodstream (primary bacteremia, 10-15%), skin and soft tissue (5-10%), and central nervous system (meningitis, encephalitis, less than 5%).

SECTION 3: EARLY RECOGNITION AND CLINICAL PRESENTATION

What are the early warning signs of sepsis?
Early warning signs of sepsis include fever above 38.3 degrees Celsius or hypothermia below 36 degrees Celsius, tachycardia with heart rate above 90 beats per minute, tachypnea with respiratory rate above 20 breaths per minute, altered mental status or confusion, new onset hyperglycemia above 140 mg/dL in non-diabetic patients, elevated white blood cell count above 12,000 per microliter or leukopenia below 4,000 per microliter, and elevated C-reactive protein or procalcitonin.

What are the early signs of septic shock?
Early signs of septic shock include persistent hypotension with systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg or MAP below 65 mmHg despite initial fluid resuscitation, serum lactate above 2 mmol/L indicating tissue hypoperfusion, decreased urine output below 0.5 mL/kg/hr for more than 2 hours, mottled or clammy skin indicating poor peripheral perfusion, sudden change in mental status or worsening confusion, and signs of organ dysfunction including rising creatinine, elevated bilirubin, or worsening coagulation parameters.

What skin changes indicate sepsis or septic shock?
Skin findings in sepsis include mottling of extremities particularly knees and elbows indicating poor perfusion, pallor or grayish discoloration, prolonged capillary refill time above 2 seconds, cold and clammy extremities despite fever, petechiae or purpura suggesting disseminated intravascular coagulation, and in meningococcal sepsis a non-blanching purpuric rash is characteristic and requires emergency treatment.

How does sepsis present differently in elderly patients?
Elderly patients may not mount a fever and can present with hypothermia instead. Altered mental status or new confusion may be the primary presenting symptom. Baseline comorbidities can mask typical signs. Tachycardia may be blunted by beta-blocker use. Functional decline, falls, and decreased oral intake can be early presentations. A high index of suspicion is essential in elderly patients presenting with any acute change in condition.

How does sepsis present in immunocompromised patients?
Immunocompromised patients including those with neutropenia may not show typical inflammatory signs. Fever may be absent or blunted. WBC may be low rather than elevated. The threshold for suspicion should be lower. Any fever above 38 degrees in a neutropenic patient constitutes a medical emergency requiring immediate evaluation and empiric antibiotics.

SECTION 4: DIAGNOSIS AND INVESTIGATIONS

What blood tests should be ordered in suspected sepsis?
Essential blood tests include complete blood count with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel including creatinine, bilirubin, and liver enzymes, serum lactate, coagulation studies including PT, PTT, and fibrinogen, C-reactive protein and procalcitonin, blood cultures before antibiotics, and arterial blood gas in respiratory compromise.

Why is lactate important in sepsis?
Serum lactate is a marker of tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism. Lactate above 2 mmol/L in the context of suspected infection indicates sepsis even without hypotension. Lactate above 4 mmol/L is associated with very high mortality and indicates severe tissue hypoperfusion. Serial lactate measurements guide resuscitation — failure of lactate to clear by 10% or more per hour indicates inadequate resuscitation.

What is lactate clearance and why does it matter?
Lactate clearance refers to the reduction in serum lactate over time with resuscitation. A clearance of 10% or more per 2 hours is associated with improved outcomes. Failure to clear lactate despite resuscitation suggests ongoing tissue hypoperfusion and may indicate the need for escalation of therapy. Lactate-guided resuscitation has been shown to reduce mortality compared to clinical assessment alone.

What is the role of procalcitonin in sepsis?
Procalcitonin is a biomarker that rises specifically in bacterial infection and sepsis. It can help differentiate bacterial from viral infection. Serial procalcitonin measurements can guide antibiotic de-escalation — decreasing levels suggest improving infection and support antibiotic discontinuation. Procalcitonin-guided protocols have been shown to reduce antibiotic duration without worsening outcomes.

How many blood cultures should be drawn and when?
At least two sets of blood cultures should be drawn from two different sites before initiating antibiotics. Each set should include aerobic and anaerobic bottles. Cultures should be drawn within 45 minutes of sepsis recognition. Blood cultures should not delay antibiotic administration beyond 1 hour — if cultures cannot be drawn promptly, antibiotics should be given first.

SECTION 5: THE HOUR-1 BUNDLE

What is the Hour-1 Bundle?
The Surviving Sepsis Campaign Hour-1 Bundle represents five actions to be completed within the first hour of sepsis recognition. These evidence-based interventions have been shown to reduce mortality when implemented together as a bundle. The bundle applies to patients with sepsis or septic shock and should be initiated immediately upon recognition.

What are the five elements of the Hour-1 Bundle?
The five elements are: first, measure lactate level and remeasure if initial lactate is greater than 2 mmol/L; second, obtain blood cultures before administering antibiotics; third, administer broad-spectrum antibiotics; fourth, administer 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate of 4 mmol/L or greater; fifth, apply vasopressors if patient remains hypotensive during or after fluid resuscitation to maintain MAP of 65 mmHg or greater.

Why must antibiotics be given within 1 hour?
Each hour of delay in antibiotic administration in septic shock is associated with a 7% increase in mortality. For every hour without appropriate antibiotics, the risk of death increases significantly. Studies have consistently shown that early appropriate antibiotic therapy is one of the most important interventions in sepsis management.

SECTION 6: FLUID RESUSCITATION

What is the recommended initial fluid resuscitation for sepsis?
The initial resuscitation recommendation is 30 mL/kg of intravenous crystalloid within the first 3 hours for patients with sepsis-induced hypotension or lactate of 4 mmol/L or greater. This is followed by reassessment of hemodynamic status and further fluid administration guided by dynamic measures of fluid responsiveness.

What type of fluid should be used for resuscitation?
Crystalloids are the first-line resuscitation fluids. Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) and balanced crystalloids such as Lactated Ringer's and Plasmalyte are acceptable. Balanced crystalloids may be preferred over normal saline to reduce the risk of hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and acute kidney injury. Albumin may be considered in patients requiring substantial amounts of crystalloids. Hydroxyethyl starches should be avoided due to increased risk of acute kidney injury and mortality.

What are the signs of fluid responsiveness?
Dynamic measures of fluid responsiveness include pulse pressure variation greater than 13% during mechanical ventilation, stroke volume variation, passive leg raise test with an increase in cardiac output or pulse pressure of 10% or more, and point-of-care ultrasound assessment of inferior vena cava collapsibility. Static measures such as CVP are unreliable predictors of fluid responsiveness and should not be used alone.

When should fluid resuscitation be stopped?
Fluid resuscitation should stop when signs of fluid overload appear, including pulmonary edema, worsening oxygenation, increasing ventilator requirements, or failure to improve hemodynamic parameters with additional fluid. Over-resuscitation is associated with worse outcomes including increased mortality, prolonged mechanical ventilation, and abdominal compartment syndrome.

SECTION 7: VASOPRESSORS

What is the first choice vasopressor in septic shock?
Norepinephrine is the first-choice vasopressor in septic shock. It acts primarily as an alpha-1 adrenergic agonist causing vasoconstriction and increasing systemic vascular resistance. It has minimal beta-adrenergic effects at standard doses reducing the risk of tachyarrhythmias. It should be initiated when MAP remains below 65 mmHg despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

What is the role of vasopressin in septic shock?
Vasopressin at doses up to 0.03 units per minute can be added to norepinephrine to raise MAP or to reduce the required norepinephrine dose. It acts on V1 receptors causing vasoconstriction independently of adrenergic pathways. Adding vasopressin may reduce norepinephrine requirements and has a vasopressor-sparing effect. It is particularly useful in refractory shock.

When is epinephrine used in septic shock?
Epinephrine may be added when norepinephrine and vasopressin are insufficient to maintain adequate MAP. It has both alpha and beta adrenergic effects providing vasoconstriction and inotropy. It can cause tachycardia and increase lactate levels independent of tissue hypoperfusion which can complicate interpretation. It is generally used as a third-line agent or when cardiac dysfunction complicates septic shock.

What MAP target should be maintained with vasopressors?
The target MAP is 65 mmHg or greater for most patients. Higher MAP targets of 80 to 85 mmHg have not been shown to improve mortality in unselected patients and may increase the risk of atrial fibrillation. However, patients with chronic hypertension may require higher MAP targets to maintain adequate organ perfusion and this should be individualized.

SECTION 8: ANTIBIOTIC THERAPY

What are the principles of antibiotic selection in sepsis?
Antibiotic selection should be broad-spectrum to cover all likely pathogens, administered intravenously at appropriate doses, initiated within 1 hour of sepsis recognition, guided by the suspected source of infection, local resistance patterns, and patient-specific factors including prior culture results, recent antibiotic exposure, travel history, and immune status.

What empiric antibiotics are used for community-acquired sepsis?
For community-acquired sepsis with unknown source, common regimens include piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g every 6 hours covering gram-positive and gram-negative organisms including Pseudomonas, or a carbapenem such as meropenem 1g every 8 hours for severely ill patients or those with recent healthcare exposure. Vancomycin 25-30 mg/kg loading dose is added to cover MRSA, particularly in patients with risk factors including prior MRSA infection, dialysis, or skin infections.

What empiric antibiotics are used for healthcare-associated sepsis?
Healthcare-associated sepsis warrants broader coverage including antipseudomonal beta-lactams or carbapenems, vancomycin or daptomycin for MRSA coverage, and consideration of antifungal therapy in patients with prolonged ICU stay, total parenteral nutrition, or prior fungal colonization. Local antibiograms should guide empiric selection.

When should antibiotics be de-escalated?
Antibiotic de-escalation should occur as soon as culture results are available, typically at 48 to 72 hours. De-escalation involves narrowing the antibiotic spectrum to target identified organisms, discontinuing agents that are no longer necessary, and shortening duration when clinically appropriate. Procalcitonin-guided protocols support safe de-escalation. Standard duration for uncomplicated bacteremia is 7 to 14 days depending on the organism.

SECTION 9: ORGAN SUPPORT

What are the mechanical ventilation targets in sepsis-induced ARDS?
For sepsis-induced respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation, use lung-protective ventilation with tidal volumes of 6 mL per kg predicted body weight, target plateau pressures of 30 cmH2O or less, and higher PEEP for moderate to severe ARDS. The ARDSNet low tidal volume strategy has been shown to reduce mortality compared to conventional ventilation. Prone positioning for 16 or more hours per day is recommended for severe ARDS with PaO2/FiO2 below 150.

What are the renal considerations in sepsis?
Sepsis is the leading cause of acute kidney injury in the ICU. Renal protection strategies include achieving and maintaining adequate MAP of 65 mmHg or greater, avoiding nephrotoxic agents including NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, and contrast when possible, maintaining euvolemia, and early recognition of AKI with serial creatinine and urine output monitoring. Renal replacement therapy is indicated for severe AKI with life-threatening electrolyte abnormalities, acidosis, or fluid overload refractory to diuretics.

What is the role of corticosteroids in septic shock?
Intravenous hydrocortisone 200 mg per day as a continuous infusion or divided doses is recommended in patients with septic shock who remain hemodynamically unstable despite adequate fluid resuscitation and vasopressors. Corticosteroids reduce vasopressor requirements and time to shock reversal. They should not be used in sepsis without shock. Dexamethasone is not recommended as it suppresses the HPA axis measurement needed to assess adrenal insufficiency.

What glucose targets are recommended in sepsis?
Blood glucose should be maintained at 140 to 180 mg per dL using insulin protocols. Tight glucose control below 110 mg per dL is associated with increased hypoglycemia and worse outcomes. Hypoglycemia below 70 mg per dL must be avoided as it is independently associated with increased mortality. Glucose monitoring should occur every 1 to 2 hours in patients receiving insulin infusions.

SECTION 10: MONITORING AND TARGETS

What hemodynamic targets should be monitored in septic shock?
Key monitoring targets include MAP of 65 mmHg or greater, urine output of 0.5 mL per kg per hour or greater, lactate normalization below 2 mmol/L, central venous oxygen saturation above 70% if central access is available, heart rate below 100 beats per minute, and improvement in mental status. Point-of-care echocardiography can assess cardiac function and guide volume status assessment.

What is the role of central venous pressure monitoring?
Central venous pressure is no longer recommended as a primary target for fluid resuscitation as it is a poor predictor of fluid responsiveness. Dynamic measures including passive leg raise, pulse pressure variation, and point-of-care ultrasound are preferred. CVP may still provide useful information when interpreted in clinical context but should not be used alone to guide fluid therapy.

SECTION 11: SEPSIS BUNDLES AND QUALITY IMPROVEMENT

What is the SEP-1 measure?
SEP-1 is a Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services quality measure requiring completion of a specific bundle within 3 hours and 6 hours of sepsis presentation. It includes blood cultures before antibiotics, broad-spectrum antibiotics, lactate measurement, and fluid resuscitation for hypotension or elevated lactate. Hospitals are required to report SEP-1 compliance as a quality metric.

What is the SOFA-based sepsis screening approach?
Clinical decision support tools incorporating SOFA scoring can automate sepsis screening by monitoring vital signs, laboratory values, and medication orders in the electronic health record. Automated alerts based on qSOFA criteria or SOFA changes can prompt early clinical evaluation and reduce time to treatment. Implementation of sepsis early warning systems has been associated with reduced mortality in multiple studies.

SECTION 12: SPECIAL POPULATIONS

How is sepsis managed differently in pregnancy?
Pregnant patients with sepsis require early aggressive management with immediate obstetric consultation. Fetal monitoring should be initiated. The most common sources are urinary tract infection and chorioamnionitis. Antibiotic selection should consider fetal safety. Delivery may be necessary as source control. Physiologic changes of pregnancy including lower baseline blood pressure and higher baseline heart rate must be considered when interpreting vital signs.

How is neonatal sepsis different from adult sepsis?
Neonatal sepsis is classified as early onset within 72 hours of birth typically from maternal pathogens including Group B Streptococcus and E. coli, and late onset after 72 hours typically from environmental pathogens including Staphylococcus. Signs are subtle and nonspecific including temperature instability, feeding intolerance, apnea, and lethargy. Empiric treatment typically includes ampicillin and gentamicin for early onset sepsis.

SECTION 13: PROGNOSIS AND OUTCOMES

What factors predict mortality in sepsis?
Independent predictors of mortality include age, number of organ failures, severity of illness scores (APACHE II, SOFA), source and virulence of infecting organism, adequacy and timeliness of initial treatment, presence of immunosuppression, malignancy or chronic organ failure, and institutional factors including ICU expertise and protocol adherence.

What is post-sepsis syndrome?
Post-sepsis syndrome refers to the long-term physical, psychological, and cognitive impairments that persist after hospital discharge. Up to 50% of sepsis survivors experience significant functional decline. Common manifestations include muscle weakness, cognitive impairment, PTSD, depression, anxiety, recurrent infections, and reduced quality of life. Long-term follow-up and rehabilitation are important components of sepsis care.
